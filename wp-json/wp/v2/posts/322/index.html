Sometime this summer, if President Vladimir Putin can be believed, Russia<\/a> moved some of its short-range nuclear weapons into Belarus, closer to Ukraine<\/a> and onto NATO\u2019s doorstep.<\/p>\n

The declared deployment of the Russian weapons on the territory of its neighbor and loyal ally marks a new stage in the Kremlin\u2019s nuclear saber-rattling over its invasion of Ukraine and another bid to discourage the West from increasing military support to Kyiv.<\/p>\n

Neither Putin nor his Belarusian counterpart, Alexander Lukashenko, said how many were moved \u2014 only that Soviet-era facilities in the country were readied to accommodate them, and that Belarusian pilots and missile crews were trained to use them.<\/p>\n

The U.S. and NATO<\/a> haven\u2019t confirmed the move. NATO Secretary-General Jens Stoltenberg denounced Moscow\u2019s rhetoric as \u201cdangerous and reckless,\u201d but said earlier this month the alliance hasn\u2019t seen any change in Russia\u2019s nuclear posture.<\/p>\n

While some experts doubt the claims by Putin and Lukashenko, others note that Western intelligence might be unable to monitor such movement.<\/p>\n

Earlier this month, CNN quoted U.S. intelligence officials as saying they had no reason to doubt Putin\u2019s claim about the delivery of the first batch of the weapons to Belarus and noted it could be challenging for the U.S. to track them.<\/p>\n
